001 : "Buzz Car" Line-Following Toy Car


About the Buzz Car

The Buzz Car was a group project to develop a line following car kit for the Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE) Design Fundamentals course. Given a fictional scenario, third-year undergraduate ECE students played the role of engineers hired by a toy company entrepreneur in groups of four, utilizing both hardware and software to solve the problem. The final deliverable was a prototype educational kit for K–12 classrooms that could be further developed into a marketable product to be sold.

A list of customer requirements were as follows:

  • prototype was to cost no more than $100
  • be able to follow a black line on a white background
  • be easy to power on and begin line-following function
  • output audio
  • give visual feedback in the form of lights
  • be battery-powered
  • PCBs are easy to see
  • have an aesthetic that appeals to the target audience (children)


Implementation

The Buzz Car was Formula-1 themed to appeal to the target audience and the entire system was split into four different systems:

  1. Motion subsystem: Controls the movement of the car using a PID closed feedback loop
  2. Sensing subsystem: Receives and processes the sensor data
  3. Output subsystem: Transmits data about the car through visual and audio
  4. Hardware subsystem: Sends power to the rest of the subsystems from the power source + the outer shell
  5. Controller "subsystem" (pseudo-subsystem): The central microcontroller communicating with and connecting data flow between the other subsystems
Each of the four group members was assigned to a subsystem that they would primarily work on. The subsystems interacted with each other through input and output to and from the subsystems.
PCBs were designed for each subsystem (including the controller "subsystem") to ensure the entire system was modular.

Output Subsystem

The output subsystem was assigned, which mainly consists of a speaker and an LCD screen. Thus, the two components were designed for (breadboarding, schematic, PCB design) and tested separately. The output subsystem was separate from the main function of the car to follow a line so there was a balance was kept between power usage and usability of each main component. This led to the 16x2 LCD and PCB mount speaker to be chosen. The LCD took more power but was larger than other screens that used less power, leading to readability of the text. Because of this, a speaker that used less power was chosen even if it meant limiting the audio functionality to a series of single tones.
